What is dyslexia?

As defined by the Mayo Clinic, dyslexia is a learning disability that causes difficulty in reading due to complications in speech sounds and setbacks in the relationship between letters and words. There are five main branches of dyslexia: Phonological, Rapid Naming, Double Deficit, Surface, Visual.


5 main types of dyslexia

Phonological Dyslexia- is characterized by the struggle with recognizing and manipulating sounds of words. Additionally, individuals face issues with phonemic awareness which causes effects in reading and writing, and spelling.


Rapid Naming Dyslexia- is characterized by difficulty with the ability to rapidly identify number, letters, or colors. Additionally, this form of dyslexia will affect individuals reading speed and how fast they can process words and sentences while reading.


Double Deficit Dyslexia- this form of dyslexia is a combination of phonological and rapid naming dyslexia. Since individuals will struggle with identifying sounds and naming speed, double deficit is known as the most sever branch.


Surface Dyslexia- is characterized by being able to sound out new words but the failure to identify familiar words automatically. It is understood that this form of dyslexia is caused by failure of the brain to quickly identify and process a word. Additionally, surface level dyslexia makes it hard to memorize words that sound different than they appear.


Visual Dyslexia- is characterized by the inability to remember words, sentences, patterners, colors, or shapes. Additionally, the connection between the eyes and brain are not working together, therefore the brain does not get the same or full picture that the eyes see impairing an individuals ability to learn how to spell and form letters.


What are two common misconceptions about dyslexia?

One of the most misconceptions about dyslexia is the relationship between dyslexia and intelligence. Many people believe that individuals with dyslexia are unintelligent and academically inferior. How this is not true there is absolutely no connection between dyslexia and intelligence, many individuals with dyslexia are extremely bright and creative they just learn differently the most students do. For example, Bill Gates is one of the most creative and smart men in the world, despite his learning disability he was able to create Microsoft which is now a worth $3.065 Trillion.


Another major misconception about dyslexia is that an individual can grow out of it. In reality, dyslexia is a lifelong disability, but with the right teaching methods, practice, and reinforcement, individuals can learn to manage its effects. Specialized schools and programs provide structured learning strategies that help dyslexic students build skills in reading, writing, and comprehension. Once these strategies become ingrained through consistent practice, individuals can apply them naturally, sometimes giving the false impression that they have been "cured." However, dyslexia has no cure, only effective strategies and techniques that empower individuals to succeed despite the challenges they face.
